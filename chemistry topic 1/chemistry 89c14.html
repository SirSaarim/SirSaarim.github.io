1.1 Global Warming & Climate Change

  • key concepts
  • the greenhouse effect
    • what is solar radiation?
      • a portion of electromagnetic radiation given off by the sun
      • consists of infrared radiation, visible light and ultraviolet radiation (UV)
      • largely made of visible wavelengths, longer infrared wavelengths, smaller component of shorter UV wavelengths
    • how much solar radiation is reflected by the earth's surface & atmosphere?
      • 30% reflected due to albedo (reflectivity) of the earth's surface & atmosphere
      • land and sea ice, snow coverage & clouds are highly reflective
    • how much solar radiation is absorbed by the earth's atmosphere?
      • approximately 22% absorbed by the atmosphere
      • a component of UV radiation is absorbed by gases present in the stratosphere, specifically by the ozone molecule
      • ozone molecule : O3
    • how much solar radiation is absorbed by the earth's surface?
      • 48% is absorbed → warms the surface
    • how much solar radiation is reradiated from the earth?
      • 17% of the 48% is reradiated at lower energies and longer wavelengths
      • longer wavelengths have lower energy than visible light
      • comply with the 'thermal infrared region' of the electromagnetic spectrum
    • how much of the thermal radiation passes directly through the atmosphere?
      • 12% passes directly through, into space
    • how much of the thermal radiation is absorbed by greenhouse gasses in the atmosphere?
      • 5% is absorbed by molecules on the atmosphere known as greenhouse gasses
      • effect - warms the earth's surface & the troposphere
    • what is the atmosphere?
      • an atmosphere is a mixture of gases that surround the stars, planets and moons
      • the earth's atmosphere - 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, <1% other gases
      • atmosphere extends from the surface of earth to the barrier of interplanetary space
    • what are the layers within the atmosphere?
      • surface of the earth
      • then the lowest layer - troposphere (contains 75% of earth's gaseous molecules)
      • stratosphere
      • mesosphere
      • thermosphere
      • outermost layer - exosphere, which merges with interplanetary space
    • what does the troposphere contain?
      • 99% nitrogen (N2) and oxygen (O2) gases
      • remaining 1% - greenhouse gases (CO2, H2O, CH4)
    • what is the sun?

      the sun is a star that emits all wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation

    • what is infrared radiation?
      • IR is electromagnetic radiation with wavelengths longer than those of visible light
      • invisible to human eye, though can be felt as heat
      • longer wavelength radiation is of lower energy & usually less harmful
    • what is the greenhouse effect?
      1. electromagnetic solar radiation in the form of visible light and UV radiation travels toward the earth
      1. atoms and molecules absorb energy in the atmosphere and surface of earth
      1. short wave radiation is re-radiated off earth's surface / atmosphere as long-wave radiation from the heated molecules in the earth's atmosphere and surface
      1. some infrared radiation leaves the earth's atmosphere and enters the interplanetary space
      1. greenhouse gases in the atmosphere absorb the rest of the radiation and re-radiate it in a random direction due to their bonding structure
      1. this traps heat closer to the earth's surface, which establishes a thermal balance and therefore a stable temperature
  • greenhouse gases
    • what are greenhouse gases
      • molecules in the troposphere of earth that can absorb and emit infrared radiation
      • this absorption & emission is vital in the maintenance of temperature range that supports life on earth
    • state the 6 common greenhouse gases
      • water vapour H2O
        • most abundant greenhouse gas
      • methane CH4
        • 25x as potent as CO2
      • ozone O3
        • harmful in the troposphere → air pollutant and major constituent of photochemical smog, damages lung tissues and breaks down polymers
        • beneficial in the stratosphere → protects life from harmful UV rays
      • carbon dioxide CO2
        • greatest contributer to the enhanced greenhouse effect
      • nitrous oxide N2O
        • constituent of photochemical smog
      • chloroflurocarbons
        • synthetically produced for aerosols and refrigerants
        • depletes ozone layer

  • natural sources of greenhouse gases

      • respiration
    • carbon dioxide

    • nitrous oxide

    • ozone

  • enhanced greenhouse effect
    • what is the enhanced greenhouse effect
      • a larger amount of outgoing infrared radiation is absorbed and reradiated to the surface, creating a thermal imbalance
      • increases average surface & atmospheric temperatures → global warming
    • what is the main cause of the enhanced greenhouse effect

      increase in the emissions of greenhouse gases from anthropogenic sources have caused these changes in earth's energy budget

      global warming refers to the phenomenon when the average surface temperature and atmospheric temperature increases as a consequence of the thermal imbalance caused by processes such as the enhanced greenhouse effect

      • enhanced greenhouse effect → result of human emissions of greenhouse gasses
      • results in large-scale shifts in weather patterns

      • variation in output of solar radiation

1.2 Photochemical Smog

  • earth's atmosphere
    • describe properties of the troposphere
      • amongst all atmospheric layers, the troposphere contains 80% of all gases
      • it is the lowest layer - closest to the earth's surface
      • oxygen and nitrogen make up 99% of the gases within this specific layer
    • what is the composition of earth's atmosphere?
    • what are the 1st and 2nd most abundant atmospheric gases?
      • nitrogen 78%
      • oxygen 20%
      • others - argon, carbon dioxide, helium
    • describe the reactivity of N2 and O2 in normal conditions

      both N2 and O2 are unreactive under normal conditions as a large input of energy is required to overcome the strong covalent bonds between the nitrogen and oxygen atoms in these molecules

  • photochemical smog
    • what is photochemical smog?
      • photochemical smog is comprised of a mixture of chemicals and airborne pollutants which react with sunlight to produce air pollution in the troposphere
      • smog → smoky fog
    • causes of photochemical smog

      primary pollutants - directly emitted into the atmosphere

      • 4 examples of primary pollutants
        • carbon monoxide - CO
        • sulfur dioxide - SO2
        • nitric oxide - NO
        • nitrogen dioxide - NO2

      secondary pollutants - formed from the reaction of primary pollutants

      • 4 examples of secondary pollutants
        • ozone - O3
        • sulfur trioxide - SO3
        • sulphuric acid - H2S04
        • nitric acid - HNO3
    • what are the two main components of photochemical smog
      • ozone & nitrogen dioxide

    • where is photochemical smog heavily prevelant?
      • densely populated cities with windless, sunny conditions where temperature inversions occur on a regular basis
        • tokyo, los angeles, beijing
    • what is temperature inversion?
      • normally, low troposphere has warmer air, this heat naturally rises above where the air is much cooler
      • temperature inversion exists when there is warm layer of air which acts a blanket, trapping the cool air below
        • this can be caused by peak traffic hours when a lot of photochemical smog is produced which is then trapped in the lower troposphere due to temperature inversion
  • nitrogen oxides
    • explain the chemical properties of nitrogen
      • exists as a diatomic molecule → N2
      • held together by a strong triple covalent bond,
        • a lot of energy needed to overcome this bond
    • high energy processes which allow for reactions of N2 with other reactants
      • these processes will release sufficient energy to break N2's strong triple covalent bond
      • anthropogenic
        • internal combustion engines
        • jet engines
        • industrial furnaces
      • natural
        • lightning
        • volcanic activity
        • bushfires
    • describe formation of nitrogen oxides - NOx
      1. heat is released during the combustion of fuel (or just in high temperatures), giving oxygen and nitrogen the energy to react
      1. nitric oxide is further oxidised during combustion to form nitrogen dioxide
  • ozone
    • describe the formation of ozone
      1. nitrogen dioxide (NO2) undergoes photodissociation in the troposphere in the presence of high energy visible light and ultraviolet radiation (hf) from the sun (sunlight)
      1. a highly reactive oxygen atom (called oxygen radical) is formed in the reaction. the oxygen radical further reacts with oxygen gas in the troposphere forming ozone (O3)
  • consequences of nitrogen oxides
    • visual pollutant

      produces the brown haze which surrounds cities experiencing photochemical smog

    • eye irritation

      react with water / moisture to produce acids that irritate eyes

    • forms acid rain
  • consequences of ozone
    • respiratory irritant
      • damages tissue lining of lungs
      • elderly, young and those prone to asthma and bronchitis are more susceptible
    • impacts plant growth
      • limits gas exchange in leaves
      • during photosynthesis, ozone & CO2 enter stoma on leaves → stomata end up closing
      • it is a strong oxidant therefore affects photosynthesis and other cellular reactions
      • increases their susceptibility to disease and extreme weather conditions such as droughts
    • polymers
      • ozone can damage elastomers (elastic polymers) → makes them more brittle and susceptible to cracking under stress
      • causes degradation of polymer chains by oxidising and breaking carbon-carbon double bonds C=C
      • less double carbon bonds → reduced elasticity
      • ozone leaves the polymer chains where C=C bonds exist, producing smaller molecules and thus reducing the strength of interactions due to dispersion forces
      • as a strong oxidant, ozone also causes changes in the chemical composition of the polymers, resulting in weaker interactions between polymer chains

      summary

      • makes them more brittle
      • reduces their elasticity
      • damages them by breaking C=C bonds
      • reduces strength of interactions between polymer chains
    • facilitates enhanced greenhouse effect

      ozone is a greenhouse gas

      • a catalytic converter is a device that is fitted to the exhaust system of a vehicle with the aim of reducing emissions of nitrogen oxides and other primary pollutants from motor vehicles

  • stoichiometry
    • what is stoichiometry?
      • stoichiometry describes the quantitative relationships between reactants and products in a chemical reaction
      • it uses the reacting mole ratio from a balanced chemical equation to determine the quantity of a material that is reacted or produced in a chemical reaction
    • procedure of solving stoichiometry questions
    • excess vs. limiting reagent
      • reactants will often combine in quantities that are not consistent with the stoichiometric ratio for the reaction
      • limiting reagent - the one reactant that is completely consumed, first reactant to run out and stop the reaction
      • excess - the reactant that is partially consumed
    • consequence of excess and limiting reagents

      too much or too little of one reagent is added → not all reagents are used up in the chemical reaction → limits the number of moles formed as a product

    • role of limiting reagent in predicting the quantity of products formed
      • the limiting reagent is completely consumed and therefore limits the consumption of other reactants
      • all moles of the limiting reagent are converted into product(s). for this reason, the quantity of limiting reagent is used to predict the quantity of product(s) formed in the reaction
    • calculating reagents
      1. create a fully balanced chemical equation
      1. calculate the moles of both reactants present
      1. calculate the mole ratio of reactants mixed
      1. compare the mole ratio of reactants mixed with the reacting mole ration (from balanced chemical equation)
      1. determine the excess and limiting reagent
        • there will be less of the limiting reagent
        • there will be more of the excess

  • titration
    • what is a titration experiment?
      • titration is a technique used by chemists to determine the concentration of a solution.
      • a solution of a known concentration (called the titrant) is used to determine the unknown concentration of a second solution (called the analyte)
    • what is the titrant?

      a solution of a known concentration

    • what is the analyte?

      the second solution with unknown concentration

    • between the titrant and analyte, what is added to the other?
      • base (titrant) is added from the burette to the acid (analyte) which is placed in the flask
      • chem reaction occurs between titrant and analyte in the reaction flask
    • what is a titre?
      • the concentration of a solution as determined by titration
      • the minimum volume of a solution needed to reach the end point in a titration.
    • what is an aliquot?
      • a small measured amount of homogenous liquid added to a solution of unknown concentration
      • an accurate volume of solution (delivered by pipette)
    • explain the procedure of a titration
      1. the precise volume of the analyte is extracted and transferred to the reaction vessel using a volumetric pipette
      1. the meniscus of the analyte is in line with the graduation mark when viewed at eye level
      1. the initial volume of the titrant is measure at eye level
      1. reaction vessel is placed beneath the burette, on top of a white tile to allow for more precise determination of the end point of a titration involving colour change
      1. the tap on the burette is opened, allowing for titrant to mix with the analyte, gradually mixing the reaction vessel
      1. tap is turned off and the flow of titrant into the reaction vessel is stopped at the end point of the reaction
      1. the final volume of the titrant in the burette is then measured at eye level
      1. the volume of titrant transferred to the analyte to achieve an end point is called a titre
      1. titrant - initial volume = titre value
      1. chemists repeat the titration procedure to acheive concordant titre values
  • volumetric glassware
    • volumetric flask
      • used to accurately measure volumes of solutions
      • also used to prepare or dilute a standard solution
      • typical uncertainty of around +/-0.12ml (lower or higher by 0.12ml)
    • burette
      • used in titrations to deliver a volume of variable quantity (titre)
      • has resolution of 0.1ml → increments of 0.1
      • typical uncertainty of +/-0.3ml
    • volumetric pipette
      • used in titrations to deliver a volume of fixed quantity (aliquot)
      • produces an accurately measured aliquot
      • typical uncertainty of +/-0.3ml
      • transfers standard solution from volumetric flask to the conical flask
  • rinsing glassware
    • rinsing the volumetric pipette
      1. rise the inside using distilled water to remove reagents from previous use
      1. now rinse the insides using the analyte solution to remove the distilled water from the pipette
    • rinsing the burette
      1. use distilled water to wash the inside of the burette
        • release the water from the bottom of the burette to ensure that the tip is clean as well
        • pour the remaining water out from the top of the burette
      1. now rinse using the titrant solution to remove any distilled water from the burette
  • endpoint and equivalence points
    • what is the endpoint?

      refers to the point at which the indicator changes colour in an acid-base titration

    • what is the equivalence point?
      • the point at which equivalent molar quantities of analyte and tirant are present in the reaction vessel
      • determined by these methods
        1. pH indicator
        1. colour change
    • how does pH indicator help determine equivalence point?
      • used to approximate the equivalence point of a titration involving the neutralisation of an acid using a base
      • the point where there is a permanent colour change is called the end-point
    • how does colour change help determine equivalence point?
      • a colour change (end-point) occurs after the equivalence point and can be reached without the addition of an indicator
      • redox reactions often involve a colour change as the oxidation number of the titrant and analyte change at the equivalence point, often producing different coloured products
    • what is the procedure for determining endpoint?
      1. indicator starts off colourless when added to the analyte
      1. as drops of the titrant are added, the indicator turns pink but returns to its colourless form
      1. nearing endpoint → pink colour becomes more persistent
        • the analyte is consumed (number of moles decreases) as more moles of titrant enter the reaction flask
      1. endpoint is reached when one last drop of the titrant turns the solution to a permanent pink
    • what are concordant values?
      • titre values that abide by the resolution of the burette
        • for example, a burette has increments of 0.1ml → each concordant titre value will not have a difference of over 0.1ml
      • usually a difference of no more than 0.1ml is accepted
        • between 20.00, 20.10, 20.20, 20.00 and 20.20
        • 20.00, 20.10 and 20.00 are the concordant values
  • precision
    • what is precision?
      • precision refers to the reproducibility of a measurement in an experiment
      • in titrations, precision refers to the reproducibility of a titre value
    • what are indicators of a highly precise set of data?
      • resulting titre values all have difference within 0.1ml
      • data points are scattered very close to or on the trendline in the graph
    • what are random errors?
      • random errors are those made by the experimenter that affect the precision of the data
      • random areas result in titre values that are greater than or less than the average titre
      • 20.00, 20.60, 20.00, 20.00, 19.60
        • average = 20.00
        • 19.60 & 20.60 are evidence that random errors have reduced the precision of the experiment
    • give examples of random errors that could affect precision
      • varying amounts of indicator used
      • not rinsing the glassware correctly during and before
      • parallax error when reading the volume of the pipette
      • different observers measuring the endpoint
      • misjudging the endpoint of the titration
    • if a lower volume of analyte is transferred to the reaction flask..
      • fewer moles of analyte
      • fewer moles of titrant are required to react with the fewer moles of analyte

        → the titre value is lower than the average in this trial

    • if a greater volume of analyte is transferred to the reaction flask...
      • greater number of moles of analyte
      • greater number of moles of titrant required to react with the greater number of moles of analyte

        → the titre value is higher than the average in this trial

  • accuracy
    • what is accuracy?
      • accuracy refers to the level of agreement between the experimentally determined value and the accepted value
      • in titrations, accuracy refers to the level of agreement between the experimentally determined quantity (mass, number of moles, volume or concentration) of the analyte and the accepted quantity of the analyte
        • experimentally determined value - 1.5%w/v
        • accepted value - 2.0%w/v
        • % error = 1.5 - 2.0 divided by 2.0
        • % error = 25% below the accepted value
        • the higher the percentage → lower the accuracy
    • what are systematic errors?
      • systematic errors are inherent in the procedure or apparatus used by the experimenter
        • can be identified by repeating an experiment
        • can only be eliminated if newer, more accurate apparatus and procedure are implemented
    • give examples of systematic errors that could affect accuracy
      • not using white tile to judge endpoint
      • inaccuracies of volumetric glassware
      • inaccurate preparation of standard solutions
      • solutions decompose overtime (NaOH, NH3, H2O2) → resulting in lower concentrations
    • what is an indicator of a highly accurate set of data?

      a low percentage difference calculated between the experimentally determined value and the accepted value

1.4 Chromatography

  • principles of chromatography
    • what is chromatography

      a laboratory technique which involves the use of a stationary phase and a mobile phase to seperate the components of a mixture based on solubility (polarity) so that each compound may be identified and analysed

      different complex mixtures have differing polarities and they can be separated based on their relative adsorption to the stationary phase and solubility in the mobile phase

      allows for the separation, isolation, purification and identification of components

      the mixture can be homogenous or heterogenous

      • chroma = 'colour'
      • graphein = 'to write'

    • who first employed this technique and why?

      first employed by scientist Mikhail Tsvet in 1900

      separated plant pigments, chlorophyll, carotene and xanthophylls from plant leaves

    • what is a mixture?

      a material that contains more than one chemical compound

      can be homogenous or heterogenous

    • what are components?

      the chemical compounds within a mixture

      can be elements, molecules or charged ions

    • what is affinity?

      refers to the tendency of a molecule to associate with another molecule

      molecules interact through the formation of intermolecular (secondary) forces of attraction

      chromatography involves the separation of the components in a mixture based on the affinity of each component for a stationary phase and mobile phase

    • what is adsorption?

      the attachment of particles to the surface of a material (stationary phase)

      not the same as absorption

    • what is the stationary phase?

      a solid, immobile material that is composed of either a polar or non-polar compound

      does not move

    • what are the determinants of efficient adsorption?

      chemical compounds in the mixture adsorb (adhere) to the stationary phase with different affinities depending on the size and polarity of the molecules of each compound

    • what do polar compounds adsorb more strongly to?

      they adsorb more strongly to a stationary phase that is composed of a polar material

    • what do non-polar compounds adsorb more strongly to?

      they adsorb more strongly to a stationary phase that is composed of a non-polar material

    • what is a mobile phase?

      a mobile phase is a fluid (liquid or gas) of a chosen polarity used to drive the separation of compounds in a mixture

      • SOLVENT = MOBILE PHASE
      • does move
      • it is allowed to pass over the mixture that is adsorbed to the surface of the stationary phase
      • composed of either a polar or non-polar compound
      • components dissolve in the mobile phase (are desorbed)

    • at what rate do chemical compounds in the mixture move?

      chemical compounds in the mixture have different affinities for the mobile phase and will move at different rates along the surface of the stationary phase

      • components that are more strongly adsorbed to the stationary phase travel at a slower rate → lower Rf value and higher retention time
      • components that are less strongly adsorbed to the mobile phase travel at a faster rate → → high Rf value and lower retention time
    • what factor determines the extent of the separation of components?
      • the strength of interactions between phases determines the extent of the separation
      • components separate due to their relative levels of adsorption to the stationary phase and in some techniques, solubility in the mobile phase
    • overall factors that influence these interactions
      • size
      • polarity / charge
  • adsorption chromatography

      involves interactions on the surface of the adsorbent material used as the stationary phase

      some methods of chromatography also involve interactions within the structure of the stationary phase between liquid phases (adsorption or partition chromatography)

  • retention factor
    • what is the retention factor?
      • the different components in a mixture move at different rates along the surface of a TLC plate
        • compounds that interact more strongly with the stationary phase move a shorter distance → low rf value
        • compounds that interact more strongly with the mobile phase move a greater distance → high rf value
        • compounds strongly attracted to the stationary phase move slower with the mobile phase → low rf value
        • compounds weakly attracted to the stationary phase move faster with the mobile phase → high rf value
      • the ration of the distance travelled by a component to the solvent front is called the retention (or retardation) factor

      weak interactions with the stationary phase (silica) and will instead move along the plate very close to the solvent front

      • non-polar substances are weakly adsorbed to the stationary phase
    • what kind of interactions do polar substances make with a polar stationary phase?

      strong interactions with the stationary phase (silica) and will slowly move along the plate, almost as though the interactions make is sticky

      • polar substance is strongly adsorbed to the stationary phase

  • thin layer chromatography TLC
    • what is thin layer chromatography?
      • involves the use of a thin layer chromatography plate (TLC plate) and liquid solvent to seperate the components of a mixture
      • it is a technique used to separate components of a mixture on the thin surface of a stationary phase
    • what is a TLC plate?
      • a TLC plate consists of a thin layer of adsorbent material (such as silica - SiO2, or alumina - Al2O3) that is coated onto an inert support material such as aluminium, plastic or glass
      • the adsorbent material is 0.2mm thick (thickness of human hair)
        • the material consists of tiny particles that are separated by empty space
        • particles - silica or alumina

    • what is the origin?

      the point where the mixture is introduced

    • what is capillary action?

      upward force due to adhesion (attraction to mobile phase)

    • what is a chromatogram?

      separated components on the stationary phase

      the dried TLC plate

    • explain the process of thin layer chromatography
      1. a pencil line is drawn near the bottom of the plate to indicate the origin
      1. a sample mixture (or series of mixtures) is spotted onto the line using a micropipette drawn from a fine capillary
      1. the spotted mixture is allowed to dry and the plate is carefully lowered into a beaker or cell that contains a small volume of solvent as the mobile phase
      1. it is ensured that the solvent sits below the origin
      1. a lid is placed on top to generate a saturated environment and reduce evaporation
      1. the solvent moves up the plate, past the mixture, due to capillary action (upward force due to adhesion) formed by the tiny gaps between the grains of adsorbent material
      1. as the solvent moves up the plate, components travel with the mobile phase and experience different levels of adsorption to the stationary phase
      1. movement distance depends on how strong the interactions are with either the mobile or stationary phase
      1. TLC plate is removed and the distance that the solvent (solvent front) and component have travelled is recorded

      by changing the type of stationary phase (non-polar or polar) and solvent chosen

    • how can colourless components be identified?

  • ion exchange chromatography IC
    • what is ion exchange chromatography?
      • an analytical technique used to remove either anions or cations from a mixture by replacing them with ions of another types
      • IC relies upon the principle of ion-exchange in an equilibrium system
      • the stationary phase in IC has a particular charge (+ -) and attracts the sample of the opposite charge
    • what is an equilibrium system?

      reactions that proceed in both the forward and backward directions occur at the same rate

      the concentrations of the reactants and products remain the same

      equilibrium can only be achieved in a closed system

      • e.g. people in the beach and people on the sand
      • there may be more people on the sand, yet it can be considered equilibirum
      • equilibrium does not mean that the quantity is the same
      • it means that if 5 people went into the water, 5 people came out onto the sand
    • what shift occurs in the equilibrium if the rate of forward reaction is increased?
      • equilibrium shifts towards the products
      • equilibirum shifts towards the right
    • what shift occurs in the equilibrium if the rate of reverse reaction is increased?
      • equilibrium shifts towards the reactants
      • equilibirum shifts towards the left
    • why does the equilibrium system shift?

      to counteract the the stress placed on a system is an external change that disrupts the equilibrium

      LeChatelier's Principle states that when a stress occurs on a system that is in equilibirum, the system will respond by shifting to counteract the stress

    • explain the equlibrium equation in IC chromatography
    • what is the stationary phase comprised of?

      resin = stationary phase

      separation occurs inside a column containing porous beads of a polymer resin

      ions on the surface of polymer resin are exchanged with ions of the same type

      anions exchanged with anions

      cations exchanged with cations

      also called an ion exchanger

      cation exchanger

      • has negative charge
      • attracts positive cations

      anion exchanger

      • has positive charge
      • attracts negative anions

    • what is the charge of the stationary phase in cation exchange chromatography?

      negative

      negative resin will attract positive mobile phase / buffer, establishing equilibrium

      • protein cations will displace the positive buffer ions and attach to the stationary phase instead
      • the anions from the protein mixture will repel the stationary phase and will be eluted out first
    • what is the charge of the stationary phase in anion exchange chromatography?

      positive

      positive resin will attract negative mobile phase / buffer, establishing equilibrium

      • protein anions will displace the negative buffer ions and attach to the stationary phase instead
      • the cations from the protein mixture will repel the stationary phase and will be eluted out first
    • in which order are different charges eluted from cation exchange chromatography?
      1. strong negative
      1. weak negative
      1. increase contration of positive buffer
      1. weak positive
      1. strong positive
      1. if some ions still intact, change pH → charge of proteins will change and they will come out
    • in which order are different charges eluted from anion exchange chromatography?
      1. strong positive
      1. weak positive
      1. increase contration of negative buffer
      1. strong negative
      1. weak negative
      1. if some ions still intact, change pH → charge of proteins will change and they will come out
    • which ions are strongly attracted / repulsed from a negatively charged resin surface?

      highly positive ions will be strongly attracted

      slightly positive ions will be less attracted

      highly negative ions will experience strong repulsion

      slightly negative ions will experience weak repulsion

    • when is an ion exchange equilibrium achieved?

      equilibrium is established between ions bound to the resin surface and ions in solution

    • which interactions remain after the solvent is eluted?

      only strong ionic interactions will remain attached to the resin after the solvent has been eluted

    • how can ions bound to the resin's surface be removed?

      ions strongly bound to the surface of the resin surface need to be removed by altering the ion concentration or pH to weaken the electrostatic interactions

      flooding the resin column with a high concentration of aqueous sodium ions will place a stress on the equilibrium system

      an exchange will occur - the sodium ions will bind to the resin's surface and the exchangeable ions on its surface will be released into aqueous solution and elute

  • absorption and emission
    • what are electron energy levels?
      • electrons in shells and subshells have a specific amount of energy
      • the energies of the electrons in an atom are called electron energy levels
    • what happens to electrons during the absorption/emission of energy?

      electrons transition between energy levels

    • what are photons?

      discrete quantised amounts of energy

      • electrons absorb and emit energy in the form of photons of electromagnetic radiation
    • what is the ground state?

      lowest energy state of an atom

      electrons occupy the lowest available energy level when an atom is in the ground state

      → ground state electron configuration

    • what is the excited state?

      highest energy state of an atom

      an atom with one or more electrons in higher energy level

      → excited state electron configuration

    • what happens when electrons absorb a photon?

      photon = energy

      → electrons transition to a higher energy level

      process called atomic absorption

    • what is required for an electron to absorb/emit a photon?

      the photon energy must be equal to the difference in energy between two electron energy levels (^E)

    • what happens when electrons emit a photon?

      photon = energy

      → electrons transition to a lower energy level

      process called atomic emission

    • explain atomic absorption and emission with diagrams
      • e- are found naturally in the ground state (lowest energy level) of an atom or ion
      • e- can be promoted to a higher energy level (excited state) through the absorption of photons
      • photons (discrete quantised amount of energy) may be supplied as heat, light and electrical energy
      • e- in the excited state are unstable and return to the ground state emitting energy as photons

    • what is the wavelength of the emitted photon dependant on?

      dependant on the photon energy which is equal to the difference in energy between two electron energy levels

      emitted photons have specific frequencies (f) and wavelengths (λ)

      f and λ of a photon is related to its energy

      • high energy = high frequency and short wavelength
      • low energy = low frequency and long wavelength
      • no two chemical elements have the same electron energy levels
    • explain the production of the emission spectra
      1. electrons get excited and full of energy. this allows them to move to higher energy levels
      1. at a higher energy level, it is unstable. as such, it moves back down, releasing energy
      1. this energy is passed through a prism, splitting it into individual frequencies
    • explain the production of the absorption spectra
      1. white light is passed through a vapour of material (the element)
      1. electrons absorb different frequencies and get excited
      1. excited electrons jump an energy level, release energy and return to their ground state
      1. after passing through the vapour, the beam passes through a prism, producing an absorption spectrum

    • what is a hollow cathode lamp?

      lamp selected for the analysis of the element under investigation

      contains...

      • inert gas (such as argon)
      • the anode
      • a hollow cathode in the element of interest

    • explain the process of AAS
      1. a voltage is applied across the electrodes

        this removes atoms from the surface of the cathode, allowing their outer-shell electrons to undergo excitation

      1. a pulsed bean of emitted radiation is directed through a window at the top of the lamp to pass directly through the long thin flame
      1. the original incident beam is also split into a secondary beam prior to passing through the flame
      1. the alternative beam is directed to the detector to establish an incident (initial, prior to the flame) reading of the light intensity
      1. a nebuliser system draws up the sample through a capillary, aspirating (spraying) it into the oxy-acetylene fuel mixture entering the flame of the burner head
      1. the heat energy from the flame quickly evaporates the solvent and breaks down the compounds in the sample into atoms in a free atomic state
      1. outer shell electrons are promoted to higher energy levels in the atom. the electrons return to lower energy levels in the atom

        this results in the emission of electromagnetic radiation. the specific wavelengths of radiation present in the emission correspond to the energy transitions undertake by electrons

        • high energy = high frequency and short wavelength
        • low energy = low frequency and long wavelength

      • the light emitted from the source is absorbed by the sample.
      • the absorbance is proportional to the concentration of the element in the sample.
      • the atomic spectrometer determines the absorbance of the sample by measuring the difference in intensity (number of photons) between the sample and reference beams of light
    • what is a calibration graph?

      method for determining the concentration of a substance in an unknown sample by comparing the unknown to a set of standard samples of known concentration

      • typically, very dilute solutions are used with quantities un the ppm or ppb range

    • what do the axes represent on a calibration graph?

      a graph of absorbance (vertical axis Y) against concentration (horizontal axis X)

    • what is the calibration graph constructed for?

      is constructed for an element of interest from a set of prepared solutions

    • explain the process of constructing a calibration graph

      the range of concentrations selected depends on the expected concentration of the element in the unknown sample

      1. a set of standard solutions are prepared for the element under investigation (have increasing concentrations, like 0ppm, 1ppm, 2ppm, etc.)
      1. blank of distilled/deionised water is also included to serve as a reference for calibration
      1. each solution is being prepared at desired concentration or created through a series of dilution of a stock solution
      1. a blank is introduced to the AAS to calibrate the apparatus and then the prepared solutions are added sequentially from lowest to highest concentrations
      1. each solution including the blank will produce an absorbance reading which will plotted on a calibration graph
      1. graph is plotted absorbance (DV – y-axis) against concentration (IV – x-axis)
      1. a line of best fit is applied to the data (typically a linear fit)
      1. unknown solution is introduced to the AAS and absorbance readings are recorded
      1. unknown concentration can then be interpolated from the constructed calibration graph
